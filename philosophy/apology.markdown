# Outline of Socrate's Defense

1. Introduction to the jury 

2. There are two charges, the old one and the new (he's being tried against the new charge).
    * It's proper to defend against the old one first, and then the second. 

3. Charge #1
    * **Socrates commits injustice and is a busybody, in that he investigates the things beneath the earth and in the heavens, makes the weaker argument the stronger, and teaches these things to others.**
    * I don't charge money, like other teachers do, therefore I'm not a teacher.
    * I don't have *superhuman* wisdom like others, but I have a sort of *human* wisdom.
        * My friend Chaerephon went to the oracle, and the oracle told him that there was no wiser human than me.
        * I treat this riddle as a call to find a wiser person: I approach other people who I think of as wise, and examine them. If they are truly wise, I can tell the gods that they were wrong.
            * I examined the **politicians**, however they don't seem to know what they are talking about. However, they think that they know, so I'm wiser because at least I know that I don't know. 
            * I then approached the **poets**, but I realized that it wasn't widsom that enabled them to compose, but some sort of natural inspiration. They say many fine things, but they also don't know what they speak of.
            * I then approached the **craftsmen**. They knew some things, but because they were good at their craft they thought that they were wise at other more important things as well.
            * Because of this occupation, I'm very poor which just proves that I'm not doing this for personal glory for the sake of the gods.
        * The young people like to see others cross-examined, and therefore began following me. 
            * They start to cross examine others as well. 
            * Those who are questioned are then angry at Socrates for teaching the youth to to this.

4. Charge #2
    * **Socrates is guilty of corrupting the young, and of not acknowledging the gods the city acknowledges, but new daimonic activities instead.**
    * Debate with Meletus to discredit the charges.
        * If I corrupt the youth, then who improves them? Meletus says that the jurors do. But there are many jurors, and only one Socrates. Now, usually there are many people, but only a select few teachers can improve a person in a skill. But here, it's the opposite? That's an extraordinary claim.
        * Also, bad people do bad things to people who are close to them. Meletus says that I'm intentionally doing bad things to the people close to me, since I'm a bad person. But why am I doing bad things to people close to me if I know that by making them bad I might be bad things in return? That's a contradiction.
        * Meletus says that Socrates does not acknowledge the gods of the city, but acknowledges daimonic activities. Furthermore, he acknowledges no gods at all. But acknowledge activies of something implies acknowledging the existence of that something. So if I acknowledge daemonic activities, then I also acknowledge daemons. But daemons are either gods or children of gods. So I must therefore acknowledge some gods.
    * "If I'm convicted, it's not because of the truth of the charges, but because of the slader and malice of many people"

5. Aren't you ashamed to have engaged in something that put you at risk of death?
    * "You're not thinking straight, sir, if you think that a man who's any use at all should give any opposing weight to the risk of living or dying."
    * The gods stationed me here in Athens like a soldier, and there I should remain "steadfast in danger". 
    * In fact, if I abandoned my post as a philosopher in Athens, I would be impious and violating the god's commandment.
    * "Fearing death ... is nothing other than thinking one is wise when one isn't, since it's thinking one knows what one doesn't know."

6. What if we let you go but on the condition that you don't philosophize and cross examine people anymore? And if you violate this, you will die.
    * "I believe that no grater good for you has ever come about in the city than my service to the god"
    * **"It's not from wealth that virtue comes, but from virute comes money, and all the other things that are good for human beings, both in private and in public life."**

7. Socrates says that he's now pleading in their defense to not commit a great wrong.
    * I don't consider death a bad thing, so I'm pleading for your defense that you don't kill me, since that would cause more harm to you than me.
    * By condemning me, you would commit a great wrong against god's gift.
    * And you also know that I'm a gift from god when you see that I neglected my own affairs in order to teach you about virtue. My poverty is my testament to my dedication to my philosophy and search for virtue.
    * Well why don't you, Socrates, never give advice in public but only privately to people? Well that's because my daemonic voice always held me back from discussing it in public, but never in private. 
        * "Anyone who really fights for what's just, if indeed he's going to survive for even a short time, must act privately not publicly".
    * I can prove my honesty and preference to virtue over death by my past deeds. 
        * Example from the council.
        * Expedition to capture Leon of Salamis.
    * Now, do you think that if I would survive for this long if kept being active in public affairs?

8. Why do people then enjoy spending so much time with me?
    * Because they enjoy seeing people who think they are wise cross-examined.
    * And surely if I corrupt the young, surely some of them would recognize that when they're older and say something.

9. Closing statement
    * Many of you here might have tried to convince the jurors with copious tears and their children to arouse as much pity as possible. I won't do any of this, even though I also have a family and children.
    * I won't do this because of reputation: it doesn't seem noble for me to do so.
    * Many of these people think they're someone, and think that if they were to put to death something terrible would happen. That makes them act no better than women.
    * I won't act to you, jurors, in any way that I think is not noble, impious, or unjust.


*Guilty verdict is returned by the jurors with a close margin. Meletus proposes the death penalty.*

10. I'm not resentful of this outcome, and I still consider myself acquitted on the charges of Meletus.

11. What counterpenalty should I propose?
    * I consider myself a good man, then I will propose a penalty that I truly deserve: free meals at the Prytaneum.
    * I won't commit injustice against anyone, including myself, so I will not announce that I deserve something bad.
    * Why don't I propose exile? That would signify that I have an excessive, irrational love of life.
        * "It's the greatest good for a man to discuss virtue every day, ... on the grounds that *the unexamined life isn't worht living for a human being"

12. Socrate's friends propose to finance a fine of 30 minas.

*The jury votes for the death penalty* 

13. You will earn bad reputation and blame for having killed me.
    * I have been convicted not for lack of arguments and evidence, but because you weren't willing to hear things that you don't like to hear.
    * I didn't try to escape death too much, becuase no one should escape death at all costs.
        * Escaping villainy is much more difficult, since it runs faster than death.
    * I accept my death penalty, since I did my best, so things *had* to turn out the way they did, which means it's probably a good thing that they turned out this way.

14. Prophecy to those who convicted me
    * Vengeance will come upon you, and it will be much worse than your vengeance upon me.
        * The young people will blame you for this, and they'll be harsher since they are younger people.
        * It would be much easier to not put down other people, but to be the best you can be.

15. Conversation with those who voted for his acquittal.
    * Ever since I left home, the daemonic sign didn't oppose me.
    * Therefore what has happened was probably a good thing.
        * This just proves that those who suppose that death is a bad thing are wrong.
    * Being dead is either: **a)** nothing, no awareness whatsoever or **b)** a migration, a change.
        * In the first case, it would be an amazing advantage, since it would be like a good night's sleep.
        * In the other case, it would a journey from one place to another, where all the other dead people are. 
            * I would get to speak and cross-examine many great people who lived before us.
    * In general, nothing bad can happen to a good man in life or death, and the gods are concerned with his troubles.
    * One favor I ask of you: punish them by harassing them like I did, especially if they care too much about wealth and if they think they are someone when they are no one.
